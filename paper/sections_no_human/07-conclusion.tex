\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

A silent optional-dependency fallback produced a complete, finite, deterministic, and semantically inactive saliency path across 16,425 controlled Pillow images (and, by code inspection, any decodable input). Replacing it with fail-fast spectral-residual saliency restored all weighted inputs, raised order's held-out association by 0.047, and lowered hierarchy's by 0.263. The latter change shows why repair must be evaluated rather than assumed to improve a result.

The multi-layer gates then detected a different cross-renderer failure: raw complexity and intensity retained strong rank signal on the second renderer, but frozen development score bounds collapsed both exported scores to a constant, rendering their rank association undefined. Development-only baseline selection also corrected an overbroad all-five-loss claim, leaving four losses and one small order advantage. Together, these results show that byte-level reproducibility, feature responsiveness, score health, and evaluation discipline answer different questions.

The contribution is a reusable semantic-health testing pattern---complementary to metamorphic and property-based testing---and a fully traced two-renderer failure case study. Deterministic visual-feature pipelines should bind hashes to computations that have first passed explicit semantic invariants at every layer that carries scientific meaning.
